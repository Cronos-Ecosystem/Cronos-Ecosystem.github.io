ETH to lInea quick start workflow

Before you start

  • Confirm you are on the official bridge UI domain (check full URL, not just logo).
  • Verify your wallet is connected to Ethereum mainnet for the deposit transaction.
  • Check current L1 gas conditions inside your wallet before submitting.
  • Ensure you have enough ETH to cover both bridge amount and L1 gas.
  • Review the exact contract address shown in the transaction preview.
  • Confirm the destination address on Linea matches your intended wallet.
  • Double-check the amount field and decimal placement.
  • Close other wallet popups and browser tabs that could confuse signatures.
  • Disable unknown browser extensions during the transaction.
  • Record the transaction hash immediately after submission.
  • Understand that L1 confirmation and L2 credit are separate stages.
Bridging is a two-phase process: the Ethereum transaction locks funds, then a message credits them on Linea. Treat each phase as independently verifiable.

Sanity checks after bridging

  • Confirm the L1 transaction shows “Success” on the official explorer.
  • Verify sufficient block confirmations on Ethereum.
  • Check that the bridge contract emitted the expected event.
  • Switch wallet network to Linea only after L1 is confirmed.
  • Search your address on the Linea explorer.
  • Confirm ETH balance increase matches bridged amount.
  • Ensure no unexpected token approvals were granted.
  • Store both L1 and L2 transaction hashes for records.
  • Re-check nonce sequence if you had other pending transactions.
If the bridge UI shows completion but the explorer does not, trust the explorer. The chain state is authoritative.

Bridging ETH to Linea moves value from Ethereum mainnet (Layer 1) into a Layer 2 rollup environment. You retain control of your private keys, but the mechanics involve smart contracts, calldata, and sequencer crediting. Each step exposes you to specific operational risks.

The primary risks are phishing domains, incorrect contract interactions, gas misconfiguration, and misunderstanding transaction status. A transaction that looks “stuck” in a wallet may actually be pending due to nonce ordering or fee settings.

Verification matters because bridge UIs abstract complexity. They can lag, fail to update, or be spoofed. The blockchain explorer shows raw transaction data: gas used, status, logs, and contract addresses. That data is your ground truth.

If you approach ETH to lInea with a verification-first mindset, you reduce the probability of loss caused by user error rather than protocol failure.

What bridge ETH to Linea means in practice

Definition: Bridging ETH to Linea means sending ETH to a designated bridge contract on Ethereum mainnet, which locks the funds and triggers a cross-domain message that credits equivalent ETH on Linea.

Mental model: Lock on L1 → wait for confirmations → message relayed → credit on L2. The L2 balance only updates after the L1 transaction is finalized and processed by the Linea system.

Your wallet address is the same hexadecimal string on both networks, but balances are separate states. ETH on Ethereum and ETH on Linea exist in different execution environments.

Bridging is not instant. Ethereum block confirmations and message relays introduce delay. UI progress indicators are informational, not authoritative.

Layer 1 confirmation secures the lock event. Layer 2 credit reflects the message being executed by the rollup. Confusing these stages leads to premature assumptions about missing funds.

Glossary you will use in this guide

  • Tx hash: Unique identifier of a blockchain transaction.
  • Receipt: Post-execution record including status and gas used.
  • Confirmations: Blocks mined after your transaction’s block.
  • Gas used: Actual computation consumed.
  • Gas limit: Maximum gas you allow the transaction to consume.
  • Priority fee: Tip paid to validators.
  • Nonce: Sequential number of your transactions.
  • Replaced transaction: A new tx with same nonce and higher fee.
  • Dropped transaction: Removed from mempool without inclusion.
  • Chain ID: Network identifier preventing replay attacks.
  • RPC endpoint: Node connection your wallet uses.
  • Explorer: Web interface for reading blockchain data.
  • Approvals: Permissions granted to smart contracts.
  • Contract address: Smart contract’s on-chain location.
To verify a bridge transaction, open the tx hash on the official explorer, confirm status “Success,” check the contract address matches official docs, and review emitted logs for the deposit event.

Step-by-step: transfer ETH to Linea network safely

At each stage you will verify four states: before submit (environment), after submit (mempool presence), after confirm (L1 finality), and after credit (L2 balance update).

Step 1

Access official bridge UI

Type the URL manually or use a saved bookmark. Avoid clicking social media ads. Phishing domains often differ by a single character.

Check TLS certificate and domain spelling before connecting wallet.
Step 2

Connect wallet on Ethereum mainnet

Ensure network is Ethereum, not Linea. If wrong, the deposit transaction will fail or be misrouted.

Confirm chain ID corresponds to Ethereum mainnet inside wallet settings.
Step 3

Enter amount to bridge

Double-check decimal placement. Consider leaving small ETH buffer for future gas.

Review the transaction preview amount carefully before signing.
Step 4

Inspect contract interaction

Your wallet will show the contract address. Compare it with the official documentation.

Match contract address character-by-character with official docs.
Step 5

Review gas settings

Check base fee and priority fee suggestions. Extremely low fees risk long pending status.

Confirm max fee per gas is aligned with current network conditions.
Step 6

Sign and submit

Sign only after reviewing all fields. Signing is authorization to broadcast.

Copy the tx hash immediately after broadcast.
Step 7

Monitor pending state

If pending too long, consider speed-up with same nonce and higher fee.

Search tx hash on explorer to confirm mempool presence.
Step 8

Wait for confirmations

Multiple confirmations reduce reorg risk. Do not switch networks prematurely.

Check confirmation count on explorer.
Step 9

Check receipt logs

Receipt should show success and relevant bridge event logs.

Ensure status is “Success” and gas used is below gas limit.
Step 10

Switch to Linea network

After L1 confirmation, switch wallet to Linea network.

Confirm Linea chain ID matches official documentation.
Step 11

Search address on Linea explorer

Use your public address, not tx hash from L1.

Confirm ETH balance increased accordingly.
Step 12

Confirm L2 transaction

A corresponding credit transaction may appear on Linea.

Inspect L2 transaction status and logs.
Step 13

Record both transaction hashes

Store L1 and L2 hashes for dispute resolution or audits.

Keep hashes in offline notes.
Step 14

Revoke unnecessary approvals

If any token approvals were granted unintentionally, revoke them.

Use explorer token approval checker to confirm allowances.

Check domain spelling, certificate validity, and avoid search ads. Use bookmarks. Inspect wallet popups carefully before signing.

Approval safety

Review allowance amounts in wallet prompt. Unlimited approvals create long-term exposure.

Use explorer token approval pages to confirm current allowances.
Unexpected approval request during ETH bridge is a red flag.

Address integrity

Clipboard malware can swap addresses. Address poisoning sends tiny transactions to trick copying wrong address.

Always compare first and last 6 characters of address before confirming.

Device hygiene

Limit extensions, avoid pirated software, and keep OS updated.

After bridging, verify balance on explorer using public address, not wallet UI alone.
